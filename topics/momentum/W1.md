## What Is Momentum Trading?

Momentum trading is the practice of buying assets that have been rising and selling (or shorting) assets that have been falling, on the premise that **trends tend to persist**.

> **Core question to always be asking:** What is the strength and direction of the current trend?

Crypto markets are particularly well-suited to momentum strategies:

- **Decentralised & unregulated** ‚Äî no central authority dampening price swings
- **Highly volatile** ‚Äî frequent news-driven movements create numerous short- and medium-term opportunities
- **Traded 24/7** ‚Äî trends can develop and accelerate at any hour

> ‚ö†Ô∏è Higher volatility means higher risk. Strict risk management is essential ‚Äî manual discipline alone is often insufficient at faster timeframes.

---

## Why It Works

### Behavioural Factors
- **Underreaction** ‚Äî investors initially underreact to new information (anchoring, conservatism bias), causing prices to drift gradually rather than adjust instantly
- **Herding & FOMO** ‚Äî later overreaction extends the trend further before eventual mean reversion

### Structural Factors
- Trend-following funds and systematic CTAs mechanically buy rising / sell falling assets
- Stop-loss cascades and liquidations accelerate moves (especially relevant in leveraged crypto markets)
- News coverage and analyst upgrades consistently lag price moves, adding fresh buyers after a trend is underway

### Empirical Backing
Formally documented by Jegadeesh & Titman (1993). Replicated across equities, commodities, currencies, bonds, and crypto. Persists despite being widely known ‚Äî partly because it's psychologically hard to execute (you're always buying something that already feels expensive).

## Strategy Types

Three core strategies are the focus for Week 1. Others (funding rate momentum, narrative/sector plays, social sentiment) follow in later weeks.

### 1. Trend-Following / Moving Average Crossovers
A fast MA crossing above a slow MA signals an uptrend; crossing below signals a downtrend.

- Classic: **50/200 MA crossover** (golden cross / death cross) for longer-term trends
- Shorter: **9/21 EMA crossover** for more responsive signals
- Time-series approach ‚Äî compares an asset's price to its own recent history

> ‚ö†Ô∏è Excels in strongly trending markets but generates frequent false signals in choppy, sideways conditions ‚Äî which crypto spends significant time in.

### 2. Breakout Momentum
Buying when price clears a significant resistance level (ATH, range highs, key round numbers) on elevated volume.

- Crypto is prone to explosive breakouts due to **short squeeze dynamics** ‚Äî forced liquidations accelerate the move
- FOMO-driven retail participation typically follows
- Volume confirmation is critical: a breakout on declining volume is a warning sign

### 3. Altcoin Rotation / Cross-Sectional Momentum
Rank assets by recent performance (7d / 30d returns) and rotate capital into the top performers.

- Capital typically flows: **BTC ‚Üí ETH ‚Üí large-cap alts ‚Üí mid-cap alts ‚Üí small-cap alts**
- Catching rotation early is one of the highest-edge plays in crypto
- Requires active monitoring and fast exits

> Best used for identifying which assets have the most relative strength during a bull market cycle.

````markdown
## Strategy Types & Indicators

Three core strategies for Week 1. Each is built around a specific set of indicators ‚Äî understanding *why* each indicator works is as important as knowing how to code it.

---

### 1. Trend-Following / MA Crossovers

**How it works:** A fast moving average crossing above a slow MA signals an emerging uptrend ‚Äî and vice versa. The logic is that MAs reflect the *average cost* of participants over a period. When short-term average price rises above long-term, recent buyers are in control.

**Why it works:** Exploits the underreaction effect ‚Äî prices drift gradually as information slowly gets priced in. MAs capture that drift after it's established, trading the middle of the trend.

**Drawbacks:** Lagging by nature ‚Äî you always enter after the move has started. Gets chopped up badly in sideways markets, generating repeated false signals.

#### üìê Indicator: Moving Averages (MA)

MAs smooth price data over N periods to filter out short-term noise and reveal the underlying trend direction. The key distinction is *how* recent prices are weighted:

- **SMA** (Simple) ‚Äî equal weight to all periods. Slower to react, better for identifying major trend structure.
- **EMA** (Exponential) ‚Äî more weight to recent prices. More responsive to new price action; preferred for momentum signals.

```python
# SMAs ‚Äî trend structure
df['SMA_50']  = df['close'].rolling(window=50).mean()
df['SMA_200'] = df['close'].rolling(window=200).mean()

# EMAs ‚Äî responsive momentum signals
df['EMA_9']  = df['close'].ewm(span=9,  adjust=False).mean()
df['EMA_21'] = df['close'].ewm(span=21, adjust=False).mean()

# Golden cross: fast MA crosses above slow MA ‚Üí bullish signal
df['signal'] = (df['SMA_50'] > df['SMA_200']).astype(int)

# Or shorter-term with EMAs
df['ema_signal'] = (df['EMA_9'] > df['EMA_21']).astype(int)
```

Common periods: `20` short-term ¬∑ `50` medium ¬∑ `200` long-term trend

### 2. Breakout Momentum

**How it works:** Buy when price clears a significant resistance level ‚Äî ATH, range highs, key round numbers ‚Äî on elevated volume. The level acts as a compression point; once broken, trapped sellers are cleared out and the path of least resistance opens up.

**Why it works:** Structural ‚Äî short squeeze dynamics unique to crypto mean concentrated short positions get forcibly liquidated as price rises, mechanically accelerating the move. FOMO-driven retail then follows. The breakout is often self-fulfilling.

**Drawbacks:** Prone to fakeouts ‚Äî price briefly breaks a level then reverses, triggering stops. Volume confirmation is essential to filter real from false breaks.

#### üìê Indicator: Volume + OBV + VWAP

Volume tells you *whether* to believe a price move. Real momentum has expanding volume behind it ‚Äî it shows genuine participation, not just thin-air price movement. Without it, any indicator signal is suspect.

- **OBV** (On-Balance Volume) ‚Äî running total that adds volume on up days, subtracts on down days. Rising OBV with rising price confirms the trend; divergence (price up, OBV flat/down) is a warning.
- **VWAP** (Volume-Weighted Average Price) ‚Äî average price weighted by volume. Used as a dynamic support/resistance benchmark, especially intraday. Price reclaiming VWAP after a dip is often a continuation signal.

```python
# Raw volume confirmation ‚Äî look for expanding volume on breakout candles
df['vol_ma']       = df['volume'].rolling(20).mean()
df['vol_expanding'] = df['volume'] > df['vol_ma']

# On-Balance Volume (OBV)
direction = df['close'].diff().apply(lambda x: 1 if x > 0 else -1)
df['OBV'] = (df['volume'] * direction).cumsum()

# VWAP ‚Äî intraday benchmark
df['VWAP'] = (df['close'] * df['volume']).cumsum() / df['volume'].cumsum()
```

> ‚ö†Ô∏è A breakout on declining volume is a red flag. High-volume breakouts are far more likely to follow through.

### 3. Altcoin Rotation / Cross-Sectional Momentum

**How it works:** Rank assets by recent performance (7d / 30d returns) and rotate capital into the top performers. Rather than comparing an asset to its own history, this compares *relative strength* across assets.

**Why it works:** In bull cycles, capital flows predictably outward: **BTC ‚Üí ETH ‚Üí large-cap alts ‚Üí mid-caps ‚Üí small-caps**. Momentum in one layer telegraphs what's coming in the next. Cross-sectional ranking captures this rotation systematically.

**Drawbacks:** Requires fast exits ‚Äî by the time a rotation is obvious, you're often buying the top of that wave. Correlations spike in risk-off moves, so everything falls together and the rotation thesis breaks down.

#### üìê Indicator: RSI (Relative Strength / Divergence filter)

RSI measures the *speed and magnitude* of recent price changes on a 0‚Äì100 scale. It works because it quantifies buying vs selling pressure over a period ‚Äî not just direction, but how aggressively each side is winning.

In rotation strategies, RSI is most useful as a **filter** to avoid buying assets that are already exhausted, and to spot **divergence** ‚Äî when price makes a new high but RSI doesn't, momentum is weakening even if price hasn't turned yet.

```python
def compute_rsi(series, period=14):
    delta = series.diff()
    gain  = delta.clip(lower=0).rolling(period).mean()
    loss  = (-delta.clip(upper=0)).rolling(period).mean()
    rs    = gain / loss
    return 100 - (100 / (1 + rs))

df['RSI'] = compute_rsi(df['close'], period=14)

# Use as a filter on rotation entries ‚Äî avoid assets already overbought
df['rsi_ok'] = df['RSI'] < 70

# Divergence flag: price at new high but RSI is not
df['price_high'] = df['close'] == df['close'].rolling(20).max()
df['rsi_high']   = df['RSI']   == df['RSI'].rolling(20).max()
df['divergence'] = df['price_high'] & ~df['rsi_high']  # potential reversal warning
```

- `> 70` ‚Äî overbought, potential correction incoming
- `< 30` ‚Äî oversold, potential bounce
- **Divergence** ‚Äî price and RSI moving in opposite directions ‚Üí weakening trend

> RSI divergence is more useful than the absolute levels alone ‚Äî a strong trend can remain "overbought" for weeks. Use divergence to detect when momentum is secretly fading.

#### üìê Indicator: MACD (Momentum confirmation across all three strategies)

MACD is useful across all three strategies as a **momentum confirmation layer** ‚Äî it shows whether the trend has genuine accelerating force behind it, or is running out of steam.

It works by measuring the *gap* between a fast and slow EMA. When that gap is widening, momentum is building. When it's narrowing (histogram contracting), the move is losing steam even if price hasn't reversed.

```python
df['EMA_12']    = df['close'].ewm(span=12, adjust=False).mean()
df['EMA_26']    = df['close'].ewm(span=26, adjust=False).mean()
df['MACD']      = df['EMA_12'] - df['EMA_26']                 # momentum gap
df['Signal']    = df['MACD'].ewm(span=9, adjust=False).mean() # smoothed signal
df['Histogram'] = df['MACD'] - df['Signal']                   # strength of momentum

# Crossover: MACD crossing above signal line = bullish confirmation
df['macd_bull'] = (df['MACD'] > df['Signal']).astype(int)

# Histogram trend ‚Äî is momentum building or fading?
df['hist_growing'] = df['Histogram'] > df['Histogram'].shift(1)
```

- **MACD Line** = EMA(12) ‚àí EMA(26) ‚Äî the raw momentum reading
- **Signal Line** = EMA(9) of MACD ‚Äî smoothed trigger
- **Histogram** ‚Äî expanding = momentum building ¬∑ contracting = fading
- Crossover: MACD above signal line ‚Üí bullish | below ‚Üí bearish

---

### Indicator Quick Reference

| Indicator | Type | Best Used For | Key Limitation |
|---|---|---|---|
| SMA / EMA | Trend | Identifying trend direction, crossover entries | Lagging ‚Äî always behind price |
| RSI | Momentum oscillator | Divergence detection, avoiding exhausted entries | Can stay extreme for long periods in strong trends |
| MACD | Trend + momentum | Confirming momentum strength, spotting fading moves | Also lagging; noisy on lower timeframes |
| Volume / OBV | Volume | Confirming breakouts, spotting distribution | Doesn't give price direction on its own |
| VWAP | Volume-weighted price | Intraday support/resistance, trend benchmark | Resets daily ‚Äî less useful for swing/positional |

> No indicator works in isolation. The standard approach is layering: use MA or price structure for *direction*, volume for *confirmation*, and RSI/MACD to gauge *momentum strength* and spot early *exhaustion*.
````

```
In-sample testing ‚Üí Stress testing ‚Üí Walk-forward ‚Üí Paper trading ‚Üí Live
```

### Data Periods

### Regime Slicing
Test across each regime deliberately:

| Regime | Periods |
|---|---|
| Bull | 2017, 2020‚Äì21 Q4, 2023‚Äì24 |
| Bear | 2018, 2022 |
| Choppy/sideways | Much of 2019, mid-2023 |
| Shock events | Mar 2020 (COVID), May 2021 (China ban), May 2022 (LUNA), Nov 2022 (FTX) |

### Walk-Forward Structure
Static backtests overfit. Use walk-forward validation:
- **Training window:** 12‚Äì18 months
- **Test window:** 3‚Äì6 months
- **Roll:** quarterly

### Target Metrics

| Metric | Target |
|---|---|
| Profit factor | > 1.5 |
| Max drawdown | < 20% |
| Win rate | > 50% |
| Avg profit | > 1.5R |

### Survivorship Bias (Altcoins)
Coins with long historical data are the ones that *didn't* go to zero. To partially correct:
- Use historical snapshots of market cap rankings, not today's top 50
- Include delisted assets where data is available
- Paid sources (Kaiko, CryptoDataDownload) have more complete datasets than CoinGecko/Messari
