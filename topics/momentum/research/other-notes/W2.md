## Entry Criteria

A momentum entry needs three things confirmed simultaneously: **direction**, **strength**, and **timing**. Nailing one or two consistently produces mediocre results — all three together is where edge lives.

> Focus on the **main body of the price move** rather than attempting to find the exact top or bottom of a trend. The first and last part of a move are the most random — trade the confirmed middle where probability is highest.

### Direction

The non-negotiable filter. Price must be on the correct side of a higher timeframe trend indicator — above a key moving average for longs, below for shorts. This establishes you are trading *with* the dominant trend, not against it.

### Strength

The move needs genuine participation behind it, not just price drifting to a level.

- **ADX above 25** — trend has enough force to sustain a momentum position
- **MACD histogram expanding** in the direction of the trade — momentum building, not fading
- **Volume above its moving average** — real buying or selling pressure, not thin-market noise

### Timing

Where within the trend you enter. Chasing extended moves after a long consecutive run of same-direction candles is the most common momentum trading mistake — buying high volatility at poor risk/reward.

Better timing approaches:
- Entering on a pullback to a key MA in an established uptrend
- Waiting for the first close back above a swing level after consolidation
- RSI pulling back to the **40–50 zone** in an uptrend — a reset signal, not a reversal signal

### What to Avoid at Entry

- Entering when ATR is spiking sharply — buying into volatility, not momentum
- Entering against the higher timeframe trend even if the lower timeframe looks strong
- Entering immediately after a shock event before the market has found new equilibrium

## Exit Criteria

Exits matter more than entries. A good entry with a poor exit loses money; a mediocre entry with a disciplined exit can still be profitable.

### Stop Loss

Should be placed at the level that **invalidates the trade thesis** — the specific price where the reason you entered is no longer true. Not at a round number, not at an arbitrary distance.

- **Initial stop** — below the most recent swing low for longs; above the most recent swing high for shorts
- **Trailing stop** — move in the direction of the trade as it progresses, never move against it
- **Volatility-adjusted** — ATR-based stops trail the swing structure and tighten when caution conditions are active

> ⚠️ In crypto, intraday wicks can be extreme — stops placed too tightly on daily bars get hit by noise rather than genuine reversals.

### Take Profit

Avoid fixed targets — you want to let winners run as far as the trend carries them. Preferred exits:

- **Trail** the stop until it gets hit naturally
- **Exit when momentum indicators weaken** — MACD histogram contracting, RSI divergence forming
- **Exit at a major structural level** — prior ATH, significant resistance zone
- **Time-based exit** — if the trade has not moved meaningfully after a set number of bars, exit regardless; prevents capital being tied up in dead positions

### Partial Exits

Scale out of a position rather than closing entirely. A common structure:

1. Close **50% of position** at 1.5–2R (where R = initial risk amount)
2. Move stop to **breakeven** on the remainder
3. Let the trailing stop handle the final exit

This locks in profit on part of the trade while preserving unlimited upside on the remainder.

## Position Sizing

Position sizing is how much capital to allocate per trade — arguably the most important decision a momentum trader makes. Too small and winning trades don't move the needle; too large and a single losing trade wipes out a streak of winners.

### Core Approaches

**Fixed Fractional** — risk a fixed percentage of total capital per trade, typically 1–2%.

```
Position Size = (Capital × Risk %) / (Entry Price − Stop Price)
```

In practice using stop losses: if your stop is 5% from entry and you risk 1% of capital, your position is 20% of capital (1 / 0.05).

---

**Volatility-Adjusted (ATR-based)** — scale position size inversely to current ATR. When volatility is high, take a smaller position; when low, larger.

```
Position Size = (Capital × Risk %) / (ATR × Multiplier)
```
 Prevents overexposure during high volatility and keeps dollar risk per trade roughly constant regardless of conditions.

---

**Kelly Criterion** — mathematically optimal fraction based on historical win rate and average win/loss ratio.

```
Kelly % = Win Rate − (1 − Win Rate) / (Avg Win / Avg Loss)
```

Maximises long term compounding but in practice can be hard to hold large position sizes produced through drawdowns.
- Use **half-Kelly** (divide output by 2)


### Key Considerations

- Never risk more than **2% of capital** on a single trade
- Scale down position size during high volatility regimes (elevated ATR, ADX < 25)
- Never let a single position exceed **20–25% of total capital** regardless of signal strength

## Adapting to Market Conditions

The same momentum strategy that produces strong returns in a bull market will bleed steadily in a bear or sideways market without adjustment. **Regime awareness is not optional** — it is the difference between a strategy that survives multiple market cycles and one that only works in one.

### Bull Market

- **Bias strongly long** — hold positions for weeks to capture full moves
  - Shorts only as short-term mean reversion trades, not trend trades
- **Wider trailing stops** — strong trends produce deeper pullbacks before continuing
  - Aggressive profit targets expecting continuation
- **Larger position sizes** capitalising on higher win rates
- "Buy the dip" mentality on pullbacks
- Altcoin rotation opportunities multiply as BTC dominance falls and capital moves outward

### Bear Market

- **Bias flips** — shorter timeframes for swing trades
-   Long trades are counter-trend and should be smaller if taken at all
- **Tighter stops** — bear market bounces are sharp and fast but reverse quickly
  - Quick profit-taking at modest targets
- **Smaller positions** recognising higher failure rates
- Selective trading on countertrend bounces only
- Altcoins largely avoided — fall harder, recover slower, liquidity dries up fast
  - Cash is a legitimate position — often the highest-returning one in deep bear phases

### Choppy / Sideways

Momentum strategies are structurally poorly suited to sideways markets — they generate signals that immediately reverse, producing a string of small losses.

- Reduce or stop momentum trading entirely
- Switch to mean reversion logic — RSI extremes, range boundaries

> The **ADX Caution_Vol flag** in the J6 strategy is designed specifically for this: ADX below 25 is the quantitative definition of a trendless market

### Regime Detection Metrics

| Metric | Bull Signal | Bear Signal | Choppy Signal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Price vs 200 MA | Above | Below | Oscillating around |
| ADX (14) | > 25, rising | > 25, rising (down) | < 25 |
| Higher highs/lows | Present | Absent | Absent |
| BTC Dominance | Falling | Rising | Flat |
| Funding rates | Positive | Negative | Near zero |
| ATR trend | Stable/falling | Elevated/spiking | Contracting |

> The single most important regime indicator is **ADX**. A strategy that only trades when ADX > 25 and sits out when ADX < 25 will mechanically avoid most of the choppy-market losses that kill momentum strategies.

## Regime Filter — Coded Implementation

> *Example from Dimi's `Regime_Filter.py` and `Simple_MA_Strategy.py`*

The `build_regime` function is a direct, coded implementation of the market conditions section above — automating regime detection into a binary signal that gates every entry.

### How It Works

Classifies every bar as either bull (1) or flat/bear (0) using a MA channel with ATR-based bands. Uses **asymmetric bands** to reduce whipsawing — harder to enter a bull regime than to exit one.

```
Upper band = MA + 1.5 × K × ATR   ← must break above this to flip bull
Lower band = MA − 0.5 × K × ATR   ← only needs to break below this to flip bear
```

**K** is a scaling multiplier controlling band width relative to ATR. With K = 1.5:

Increasing K → wider bands, fewer flips. Decreasing K → tighter, more sensitive.

**Regime parameters daily:** MA 20 · ATR 14 · K 1.5


### How It Is Used in the Strategy

Regime acts as a **gate on entries**. MA crossover alone is not enough — regime must also be bull:

```python
bull  = (regime_hourly == 1)
enter = (ma_fast > ma_slow) & bull   # both must be true simultaneously
```

- **MA crossover hourly** (fast 20 / slow 190 on **hourly** bars) — entry timing signal

Exit occurs when **either** condition fails:

```python
if (not bool(bull.loc[t])) or trail_hit:
    in_position = 0
```

**Trailing stop:** 50% from the highest close since entry, updating every bar automatically:

```python
if c > peak:
    peak = c                             # ratchets up, never down
trail_hit = c < (1.0 - trail) * peak    # exit if 50% below current peak
```

The strategy is **long-only** — trades in bull regimes, goes completely flat in bear regimes.

### Connection to Market Condition Adaptation

| Manual rule | Coded equivalent |
|---|---|
| "Stop trading in choppy markets" | Regime = 0, `enter` never fires |
| "Exit when bear regime begins" | `not bool(bull.loc[t])` triggers exit immediately |
| "Wider stops in bull markets" | 50% trailing stop — stays in through deep pullbacks |
| "Use ATR to detect regime" | ATR bands define the flip thresholds |

> **Note for team:** The `ADX Caution_Vol` flag in the J6 strategy serves the same conceptual purpose as the regime filter — both identify whether the market is trending enough to trade momentum. The regime filter operationalises this into a clean binary gate rather than a caution modifier on stop distance.
